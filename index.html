How music streaming began in the 1990s

RealAudio — The RealAudio website from 1997 is a great example of how early streaming technology looked and functioned long before modern services like Spotify or YouTube existed. RealAudio, created by Progressive Networks, is considered one of the first real streaming audio services because it allowed people to listen to sound over the internet in real time instead of downloading entire files. When you look at the 1997 homepage, you can immediately see how the site was built around this new idea of “live and on-demand audio and video.” The page advertises the RealPlayer software, both the free version and the paid RealPlayer Plus, which users needed to actually play any audio stream. There are also links for web developers to download servers, encoders, and other tools, showing that RealAudio wasn’t just for listeners; it was also a whole infrastructure that websites could adopt to host their own audio or even early video streams. The site also highlights media partnerships with groups like NPR, ABC, C-SPAN, and Live Concerts Online, which suggests that major organizations were already using RealAudio to broadcast news, interviews, and live events online.

This page also reflects what people were using RealAudio for back in the late 90s. Most users were on slow dial-up connections, so the idea of streaming anything, even low-quality audio—felt new and exciting. People would download the RealPlayer, click a link, wait for the audio to “buffer,” and then listen to things like news coverage, interviews, concerts, or radio-style shows directly through the internet. The site even promoted early video streaming through RealVideo, including short films by well-known directors and coverage of events like the Tibetan Freedom Concert. For many people back then, RealAudio was the first time the internet felt like a place where you could experience live media instead of just reading text and looking at static pictures. Overall, this 1997 website captures a moment when streaming was just starting to take shape, and you can really see how RealAudio laid the groundwork for the media-rich internet we use today.

While RealAudio introduced the basic idea of listening to media online, its technology still had major limitations. Napster expanded on that foundation by shifting from streaming to direct MP3 sharing, opening the door to a completely new way of accessing music. The 1999 Napster website shows just how different music sharing looked at the end of the decade compared to the early streaming attempts like RealAudio. Napster wasn’t a streaming service—it was a peer-to-peer MP3 sharing platform, but it completely changed how people discovered and listened to music online. The homepage proudly calls Napster “the world’s largest MP3 music library,” which reflects how massive and fast-growing its userbase was at the time. Instead of hosting music itself, Napster connected millions of users directly to each other’s MP3 collections, creating a gigantic decentralized library where you could search for almost any song and download it instantly. The site encourages visitors to “Download Napster 2.0 Beta,” which shows how quickly the software was evolving as more people joined. The page also promises that with Napster, you’ll “never come up empty handed when searching for your favorite music again,” which captures how new and exciting the idea of unlimited digital access to music felt in 1999.

When people used Napster back then, the experience was completely different from streaming services today. Users would download the Napster application, log in, and search for songs they wanted. The program would show other users currently online who had those MP3 files available, and you could download directly from their computer. Because MP3 files were small, even someone on a dial-up connection could build a huge digital music library fairly quickly. It’s also clear from the website that Napster presented itself almost like a tech startup, with links to “Jobs,” “Company,” and “Inside Scoop on the Product,” which shows how it tried to frame what they were doing as innovative rather than illegal. Even though Napster wasn’t streaming in the modern sense, it played a major role in shaping the expectations people would eventually have for music online instant access, huge selection, and the ability to discover new songs without buying CDs. Looking at this 1999 homepage now, compared to other audio services at the time, you can really see how Napster marked the beginning of a major shift in digital music culture.

Expert Analysis

Steve Jones, “Music and the Internet,” Popular Music, 2000 — This article helps explain the larger shift happening in the music world during the late 1990s that made technologies like RealAudio and Napster possible. Steve Jones argues that while recording technology had long been the core of the music industry, power was rapidly moving toward distribution once music began circulating through digital networks instead of physical formats like CDs. He organizes this change into three areas that mirror the developments seen on this site: music production, music consumption, and music distribution.

For production, Jones explains that musicians were beginning to use the internet as a creative tool, collaborating across long distances, sharing digital files, and producing music with new software and computer-based tools. These changes made it easier for music to exist in digital form, which directly supported early streaming experiments like RealAudio and later large-scale file sharing through Napster.

In terms of consumption, Jones focuses on how listeners were changing the way they experienced music online. People were downloading files, participating in fan communities through Usenet, IRC, and mailing lists, and increasingly discovering music on personal computers rather than through record stores or traditional radio. This shift from public listening spaces to private, computer-based listening connects closely to both RealAudio’s early streaming model and Napster’s focus on personal digital music libraries.

For distribution, Jones describes the major disruption caused by digital networks. He discusses early online retailers, MP3 downloading, piracy concerns, copyright debates, and emerging streaming technologies like RealAudio and Liquid Audio. He explains that the music industry feared losing its role as a middleman, a process known as disintermediation, as technology companies began to play a larger role in how music moved across the internet. This context helps explain why Napster became so influential and why it triggered such a strong response from record labels.

Reebee Garofalo, "From Music Publishing to MP3: Music and Industry in the Twentieth Century," American Music, 1999 - Building on the shift toward digital music distribution seen with RealAudio and Napster, this article focuses more closely on the MP3 format that powered much of that change.

This article provides important context for understanding why technologies like Napster emerged so powerfully at the end of the 1990s. It explains how the MP3 format, which was developed in the early 1990s, became widely influential once personal computers and internet connections were fast enough to support regular downloading. By the late 1990s, MP3 files were small, easy to share, and compatible with popular software like Winamp and RealJukebox, allowing users to store large amounts of music digitally. This shift made music far more portable and personal, reinforcing the move away from physical formats that had already begun with early streaming technologies like RealAudio.

The article also emphasizes how MP3 technology challenged the traditional music industry by enabling direct distribution between artists and listeners. Instead of relying on record labels, artists could upload their music online, and fans could share it freely across the internet. This environment made services like Napster not only possible but inevitable. Napster did not invent MP3 sharing, but it organized and simplified a process that millions of people were already participating in. The scale of MP3 downloading by 1999, with millions of files exchanged daily, shows how quickly users embraced this new model of access.

In addition to software, the article discusses how new hardware such as early portable MP3 players further changed listening habits. Devices like the Rio allowed users to carry digital music with them, reinforcing the idea that music no longer needed to exist on physical media. Legal battles over these devices reveal how unprepared the industry was for this shift. Efforts to control digital music through security standards struggled to keep pace with technological growth, much like the industry’s response to Napster itself.

Overall, this article helps connect the progression from early streaming experiments like RealAudio to the explosive popularity of Napster. It shows how improvements in digital compression, software, and hardware reshaped music consumption and distribution throughout the 1990s. Together with the other sections of this site, it illustrates how the decade laid the foundation for the streaming-based music culture that exists today.

How music streaming has since evolved

Modern day Audio streaming — APNEWS, "It’s time to unpack Spotify Wrapped. Here’s how the music streamer compiled your 2025 recap," 2025.

Today, music streaming through platforms like Spotify and Apple Music looks completely different from the early experiments of the 1990s, but it builds directly on the same ideas that services like RealAudio and Napster introduced. Modern streaming platforms provide instant access to millions of songs on demand, but they also track detailed listening data and use it to shape how users experience music. The AP article on Spotify Wrapped shows how streaming has evolved into something highly personalized. For better or for worse, listening habits are collected throughout the year and transformed into summaries that tell users what they listened to most, how long they listened, and even how their taste compares to others. Features like Wrapped and Apple Music Replay turn data tracking into entertainment, making listening a social and shareable experience rather than just a private activity. This connects back to the late 1990s shift described throughout this site, when music moved onto computers and distribution became more important than physical formats. While early platforms focused on simply making digital music possible, modern services now focus on analyzing, organizing, and presenting that access in ways that shape identity and community. In that sense, Spotify and Apple Music represent the end result of trends that began in the 1990s, when the internet first changed how people found, shared and related to music.
